Career

In her early twenties, Davies maintained her commitment to traditional Welsh textile patterns, but her artistic vision was radically expanded after attending a lecture by William Morris in London in the early 1850s. Morris‚Äôs views on the dignity of labor, the moral value of beauty in everyday life, and the rejection of industrial mass production deeply resonated with Davies‚Äôs social conscience. However, unlike many in the Arts and Crafts movement who rejected industrial themes entirely, Davies sought to weave the dual realities of Wales together ‚Äî honouring both its ancient crafts and its modern industries. Her work visually expressed the industrial transformation of the Welsh landscape without romanticising or denying it. She created patterns that acknowledged the dignity of labor, the hardship of miners, and the stark beauty of machinery against the hillsides.[4]

By the 1870s, Eluned Davies had established herself as a significant figure in Welsh applied arts. She produced commercial weaving designs for several mills, blending traditional Welsh doublecloth techniques with her innovative motifs.

Personal life
